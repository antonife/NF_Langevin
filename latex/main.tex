\begin{abstract}
Coarse-grained descriptions of driven stochastic systems systematically underestimate entropy production by discarding intra-state fluctuations.
We present a normalizing-flow approach that learns the full continuous probability density $p(x,t)$ from ensemble trajectory data, enabling computation of the microscopic entropy production rate (EPR) via the Fokker--Planck probability current without coarse-graining.
We apply the method to an overdamped Langevin particle in a periodically tilted bistable Landau potential $V(x,h) = x^4 - 2x^2 + hx$.
Using stacked one-dimensional planar flows with a Gaussian mixture base distribution, we demonstrate that the flow-based EPR recovers the full Sekimoto dissipation ($\Sigma_{\mathrm{NF}} \approx \Sigma_{\mathrm{Sek}}$), whereas a two-state Schnakenberg projection captures only ${\approx}\,5\%$~of the total.
We show analytically and numerically that a Gaussian mixture base is essential for bimodal targets: monotone composition of a unimodal base cannot capture multimodal densities regardless of network depth.
A force-free extension computes the probability current directly from the continuity equation $\partial p/\partial t = -\partial J/\partial x$, using only the NF-learned densities and a Kramers--Moyal estimate of the diffusion coefficient, achieving ${\approx}\,11\%$ accuracy without knowledge of the force field.
The method is implemented in pure NumPy/SciPy without automatic differentiation, making it accessible for pedagogical and small-scale research applications.
\end{abstract}
